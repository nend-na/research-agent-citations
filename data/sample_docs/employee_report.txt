Employee Attrition Report - Annual HR Findings

Compensation remains one of the strongest predictors of voluntary turnover. Employees who feel underpaid relative to market rates are significantly more likely to leave within twelve months of a compensation review. Surveys across five companies found that pay dissatisfaction was cited by 41 percent of exiting employees as a primary reason for resignation.

High workload and burnout also contribute significantly to attrition. Teams reporting sustained overtime of more than ten hours per week showed a 28 percent higher exit rate compared to teams with balanced workloads. Burnout was especially pronounced in customer-facing and engineering roles during periods of rapid company growth.

Limited advancement opportunities are strongly associated with employee exits. Employees who did not receive a promotion or role change within three years were twice as likely to leave voluntarily compared to employees who had a clear growth path. Career stagnation was the second most commonly cited reason for departure in exit interviews.

Poor manager relationships and lack of recognition were also frequently mentioned. Employees who rated their manager relationship poorly were three times more likely to leave within a year.

Finally, misalignment between personal values and company culture contributed to attrition among newer hires, particularly those who joined during rapid scaling phases and felt the culture shifted after onboarding.
