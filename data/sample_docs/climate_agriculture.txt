Climate Impacts on Agriculture - Evidence Overview

Rising average temperatures have been shown to reduce yields for staple crops such as wheat, maize, and rice in many growing regions. Studies estimate that each degree Celsius of warming above historical baselines can reduce maize yields by several percentage points in regions without adequate irrigation infrastructure.

Changes in precipitation patterns, including more frequent droughts and more intense but less predictable rainfall, have disrupted planting and harvest cycles. Farmers in semi-arid regions report increasing difficulty predicting optimal planting windows, which has led to lower germination rates in some seasons.

Extreme weather events, including heatwaves and flooding, have caused direct crop damage and soil degradation. Flooding in low-lying agricultural regions has been linked to increased soil salinity, which reduces long-term land productivity.

Pest and disease pressure on crops has also shifted as warming temperatures allow some pests to expand into new geographic ranges or extend their breeding seasons. This has increased reliance on pesticides in regions that previously had lower pest pressure.

Adaptation strategies such as drought-resistant crop varieties, improved irrigation efficiency, and shifting planting calendars have shown some success in offsetting yield losses, though adoption of these strategies remains uneven across regions with different levels of agricultural investment and infrastructure.
