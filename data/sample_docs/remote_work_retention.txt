Remote Work and Employee Retention - Research Summary

Flexible work arrangements have been consistently linked with increased retention across multiple industries. A multi-year study of technology and professional services firms found that employees with hybrid or fully remote options were 23 percent less likely to leave their employer within two years compared to employees required to work fully on-site.

Autonomy over work location and schedule was identified as a key driver of job satisfaction. Employees reported higher engagement scores when given control over when and where they completed their work, particularly for roles that did not require constant in-person collaboration.

However, remote work is not a universal solution. Employees in fully remote arrangements without strong onboarding support reported higher feelings of isolation, and isolation was correlated with lower long-term retention in some cohorts, especially among employees in their first year of tenure.

Companies that combined remote flexibility with intentional community-building activities, regular one-on-one manager check-ins, and clear communication norms saw the strongest retention outcomes. The data suggests that flexibility alone is insufficient; it must be paired with deliberate efforts to maintain connection and clarity of expectations.

Compensation and career growth still outweighed remote work flexibility as the top two retention factors in most surveyed organizations, but flexibility remained a significant secondary factor, particularly for employees with caregiving responsibilities.
